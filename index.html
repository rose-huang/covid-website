Methods & Demographics

To acquire the data used for this visualization, we conducted a 10-question survey for college juniors and seniors. The questions asked for basic demographic information, employment status and impact, and sentiment regarding graduate school, economic outlook, and mental health due to COVID-19. The survey was released on April 16th, 2020, and the last responses used in the visualization were taken from April 29th, 2020. The survey was shared primarily through social media to students at Columbia University, Princeton University, UC Berkeley, and Georgetown University among other universities.

Of the 229 survey respondents, 61 are currently juniors and 167 are seniors during the 2019-2020 school year. Science, Technology, Engineering, or Math (STEM) was the most commonly indicated area of study, followed by Humanities, and then Economics, Business, or Finance. The most popular industry was Finance, Consulting, or Business with Technology or Software being a close second.

Comparison to 2008 Recession

College graduates in the Great Recession entered the workforce to mass layoffs and frozen hiring. During the current pandemic, upperclassmen are experiencing many of the same difficulties as companies are forced to cancel internship programs and rescind full-time offers.

Graduates in a recession suffer from higher unemployment rates and lower salaries for 10 to 15 years after exiting school — an effect known as "scarring" (NYT). According to NCES, students who graduated in 2008 were roughly twice as likely to be unemployed as students who graduated in 1993 or 2000. The median salary for humanities majors in 2008 was $30,000, compared to a median of $35,000 for graduates in 2001. Of those graduates of 2008 who did find work, 27% reported working in a field unrelated to their major, compared to 22% of graduates in 2001 and 24% of graduates in 2004. This sentiment is echoed in our survey responses, as students express changing industries due to a desire for financial security and job stability.

I was going for entertainment technology, but since that's shut down all of the employers that have gotten back to me are no longer hiring. Now I'm looking at more manufacturing based engineering work.

I am now looking into lower paying jobs and jobs in sectors I wasn't originally interested in due to a need for financial stability in the midst of this economic crisis.

I'm worried about finding jobs with my F-1 visa and I also plan to go deeper into tech for job security - goal changing from PM to potential SWE

The number of new job listings posted between mid-Feb and mid-March dropped 29% compared to same period in 2019. Postings for retail stores dropped 14%, events jobs dropped 20% and casino/hotel jobs dropped 23%. Conversely, some industries have actually seen an increase in listings, with e-commerce listings rising 228% over the month of March and personal consulting jobs increased 26% (NYT)

Graduates in 2008 also pursued post-baccalaureate degrees at higher rates than their predecessors, with 22% of graduates getting advanced degrees compared to 18% and 21% of graduates in 1994 and 2001, respectively. SURVEY STATS ON CONSIDERING GRAD SCHOOL

Researchers note that there are several factors that could impact the extent of the scarring effect for current seniors, like the duration of the pandemic and the severity of the recession that is bound to follow it (NYT). The effects could also extend to the Class of 2021, many of whom have had their summer internships cancelled or shortened. For many students, these internships are key opportunities to build their network and often lead to a full-time offer of employment after graduation.
